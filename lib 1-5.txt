INTRODUCTION
1.1 Background of the Study
Libraries are the cornerstone of academic institutions, serving as vital hubs for knowledge dissemination, research support, and intellectual growth. They provide students, faculty, and researchers with access to a wide range of resources, including books, journals, databases, and digital materials, fostering academic excellence and innovation (Smith & Johnson, 2020). According to Adebayo et al. (2022), academic libraries enhance learning outcomes by offering curated collections and services that support curriculum delivery, research projects, and lifelong learning. Beyond resource provision, libraries facilitate collaboration, critical thinking, and information literacy, which are essential for preparing students for a rapidly evolving global landscape (Gupta & Sharma, 2021). The John Harris Library plays a pivotal role in supporting the university's academic mission, but its outdated Library Management System (LMS) limits its ability to meet modern user expectations, necessitating an enhanced system.
Libraries also contribute to institutional reputation and student satisfaction. A well-equipped library with efficient systems can attract prospective students and faculty, as it signals a commitment to academic quality (Wilson & Carter, 2023). However, inefficiencies such as slow resource retrieval, limited digital access, and manual processes can hinder user experience, leading to reduced engagement (Choi & Lee, 2023). For the University of Benin, modernizing the John Harris Library's LMS is critical to maintaining its academic standing and supporting its diverse user base, including students, faculty, and staff.
1.1.2 Current Trends in Library Automation and Management
The landscape of library management has undergone significant transformation due to advancements in technology. Library automation, which involves the use of software to manage library operations, has shifted from basic cataloging to comprehensive systems that integrate digital resources, analytics, and user-centric features (Kumar & Singh, 2021). Cloud-based LMS have become increasingly popular, offering scalability, remote access, and reduced maintenance costs compared to traditional on-premise systems (Smith & Johnson, 2020). These systems enable real-time synchronization of data, allowing users to access resources from any location, a feature particularly valued by students and faculty in academic settings (Adebayo et al., 2022).
Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) are emerging trends in library automation, enabling features like personalized resource recommendations and predictive analytics for inventory management (Choi & Lee, 2023). For instance, AI-driven systems can analyze user behavior to suggest relevant materials, enhancing discoverability and engagement (Gupta & Sharma, 2021). Radio Frequency Identification (RFID) technology is also gaining traction for automating check-in/check-out processes and inventory tracking, reducing manual errors and improving efficiency (Patel & Kumar, 2022). Mobile accessibility is another critical trend, with studies showing that mobile-friendly LMS increase user engagement by up to 25% (Adebayo et al., 2022).
Additionally, the integration of LMS with university systems, such as Single Sign-On (SSO) authentication and learning management systems (e.g., Canvas, Blackboard), has become a priority to streamline user access (Kumar & Singh, 2021). Data analytics tools are also transforming library management by providing insights into resource usage, circulation patterns, and user preferences, enabling data-driven decision-making (Wilson & Carter, 2023). These trends highlight the need for an enhanced LMS at the University of Benin that incorporates modern technologies to improve efficiency and user satisfaction.

1.6 Definition of Terms
Library Management System (LMS): A software platform designed to automate library operations, including cataloging, circulation, user management, and resource tracking, to enhance efficiency and user access (Kumar & Singh, 2021).
Library Automation: The use of technology to streamline library processes, such as cataloging, borrowing, and inventory management, reducing manual tasks and improving service delivery (Smith & Johnson, 2020).
Cloud-Based LMS: A library management system hosted on remote servers, providing scalability, remote access, and reduced maintenance costs compared to on-premise systems (Choi & Lee, 2023).
Artificial Intelligence (AI): The application of intelligent algorithms in LMS to enable features like personalized recommendations, predictive analytics, and automated cataloging (Gupta & Sharma, 2021).
Radio Frequency Identification (RFID): A technology using radio waves to automate check-in/check-out, inventory tracking, and theft prevention in libraries, improving operational efficiency (Patel & Kumar, 2022).
Single Sign-On (SSO): An authentication mechanism that allows users to access multiple systems, including the LMS, with a single set of login credentials, enhancing user convenience (Kumar & Singh, 2021).
Information Literacy: The ability to locate, evaluate, and use information effectively, supported by library resources and services to foster critical thinking and research skills (Adebayo et al., 2022).
User-Centric Design: The approach to designing LMS interfaces and functionalities based on user needs, preferences, and accessibility requirements to maximize engagement and satisfaction (Gupta & Sharma, 2021).

LITERATURE REVIEW
2.1 Introduction
This chapter provides a synthesis of studies sourced from academic databases and peer-reviewed journals. The review examines the historical evolution of LMS, analyzes the features and limitations of contemporary systems, explores emerging technologies, and evaluates relevant case studies. 2.2 Overview of Library Management Systems
Library Management Systems have undergone a significant transformation from manual, card-based catalogs to sophisticated digital platforms. As Kumar and Singh (2021) point out, early LMS focused on basic cataloging and circulation, while modern systems use cloud computing, mobile accessibility, and real-time analytics to manage both digital and physical resources. This change is in response to the growing demand for seamless access to e-books, journals, and other digital content in academic libraries (Smith & Johnson, 2020). The adoption of open standards like MARC21, Z39.50, and Dublin Core has also improved interoperability, allowing libraries to connect with external databases (Choi & Lee, 2023).
The shift to web-based LMS has revolutionized library operations by allowing remote access and reducing the need for physical infrastructure (Adebayo et al., 2022). For example, these systems enable users to search catalogs, reserve resources, and manage their accounts from any device, which improves convenience and accessibility (Wilson & Carter, 2023). However, many academic libraries, especially those with limited budgets, still rely on older systems that lack these features. This leads to issues like slow retrieval times, manual processes, and poor user interfaces (Patel & Kumar, 2022). The John Harris Library faces similar challenges, as its outdated LMS makes it difficult to provide efficient services.
Modern LMS are also heavily influenced by user expectations. Gupta and Sharma (2021) highlight that students and faculty want intuitive interfaces, seamless integration with other university systems (e.g., single sign-on), and mobile compatibility. The move to cloud-based solutions has been driven by the need for scalability and cost-effectiveness, especially for mid-sized institutions (Kumar & Singh, 2021). Furthermore, the integration of analytics tools helps libraries track resource usage and optimize operations (Smith & Johnson, 2020). These trends underscore the need for a new LMS at the John Harris Library that aligns with technological advancements and user needs to ensure efficient management of its diverse collection.
The evolution of LMS has also been influenced by global trends in library automation. For instance, the use of open-source platforms has made advanced systems more accessible, allowing smaller libraries to implement robust solutions without high costs (Adebayo et al., 2022). However, the complexity of customizing these systems can strain limited IT resources, which is a concern for institutions like Uniben (Wilson & Carter, 2023). Additionally, the increasing focus on digital equity requires that LMS support diverse users, including those with disabilities, through accessible interfaces and multilingual features (Choi & Lee, 2023). The proposed LMS must address these evolving requirements to remain relevant in the long term.
2.3 Existing Library Management Systems
Academic libraries use a variety of LMS, both open-source and commercial, each with distinct features and limitations. An in-depth analysis of these systems is crucial for designing the proposed LMS for the John Harris Library.
Open-source systems like Koha and Evergreen are popular because they are affordable and flexible. Koha, which is widely used, offers strong modules for cataloging, circulation, and reporting, making it a good fit for academic libraries (Adebayo et al., 2022). Its open-source nature allows for customization, but this often requires significant technical expertise, which can be a barrier for institutions with limited IT staff (Wilson & Carter, 2023). Evergreen, designed for library networks, excels at scalability and resource sharing across multiple locations but lacks advanced mobile integration and user-friendly interfaces, making it less appealing for smaller libraries (Choi & Lee, 2023). While both are cost-effective alternatives to commercial solutions, they require ongoing maintenance and updates, which must be considered by the John Harris Library.
Commercial systems like SirsiDynix Symphony offer strong security and analytics but require a significant investment in infrastructure, which may not align with budget constraints (Patel & Kumar, 2022). Libsys 10, a cloud-based commercial system, provides scalability and remote access but is often criticized for its complex user interfaces, which require extensive training (Gupta & Sharma, 2021).
Despite their strengths, these systems have notable limitations. Gupta and Sharma (2021) note that many systems have outdated interfaces that don't meet modern usability standards, leading to low user engagement, especially among students who are used to intuitive digital platforms. Complex navigation and a lack of mobile compatibility deter users who prefer to access resources on their smartphones (Adebayo et al., 2022). Integration challenges, such as limited compatibility with university authentication systems (e.g., single sign-on) or external databases, also create access barriers (Kumar & Singh, 2021). Finally, security vulnerabilities in older systems, including weak encryption, pose risks to user data privacy, which is a growing concern (Patel & Kumar, 2022).
A comparative analysis of LMS shows a trade-off between cost, functionality, and scalability. Open source systems are affordable but require technical expertise, while commercial systems offer advanced features at a higher cost (Wilson & Carter, 2023). Hybrid approaches, which combine the flexibility of open source with select commercial features like cloud hosting, have been suggested as a solution for mid-sized libraries (Adebayo et al., 2022). For the John Harris Library, a customized LMS that balances cost and functionality, with a focus on usability and integration, is essential to address current limitations and meet user expectations.
2.4 Technologies in Modern Library Management
Emerging technologies have transformed LMS, enabling libraries to improve efficiency, accessibility, and user experience. Below is a detailed look at key technologies relevant to the proposed LMS.
Cloud-based LMS have become a cornerstone of modern library management because of their scalability, cost-effectiveness, and remote accessibility. Smith and Johnson (2020) note that cloud solutions reduce the need for on-premise servers, which lowers maintenance costs and improves system uptime. Cloud systems also enable real-time data synchronization, allowing users to access resources from any location (Kumar & Singh, 2021). For instance, they support features like real-time availability checks and remote reservations, which are critical for busy academic libraries (Adebayo et al., 2022). However, challenges like internet dependency and data security risks require strong infrastructure and encryption protocols (Choi & Lee, 2023). A cloud-based LMS for the John Harris Library could minimize hardware costs while ensuring the system can scale for future growth.
Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML): AI and ML are revolutionizing LMS by enabling features like predictive analytics, personalized recommendations, and automated cataloging. Gupta and Sharma (2021) highlight that AI-driven recommendation systems improve resource discoverability by suggesting materials based on a user's preferences and borrowing history. AI can also analyze user search patterns to recommend relevant e-books or journals, which enhances the user experience (Adebayo et al., 2022). ML algorithms can optimize inventory management by predicting demand and identifying underutilized materials (Wilson & Carter, 2023). AI-powered chatbots can also help users with search queries and account management, reducing the workload for staff (Choi & Lee, 2023). However, implementing AI requires significant resources and expertise. A phased approach to AI integration would be more feasible for the John Harris Library, starting with basic features like recommendations.
Radio Frequency Identification (RFID): This technology is increasingly used for automated check-in/check-out, inventory tracking, and theft prevention. Patel and Kumar (2022) report that RFID systems can reduce manual errors by up to 40% and streamline circulation processes. For example, RFID-enabled self-checkout kiosks allow users to borrow and return materials on their own, improving efficiency and autonomy (Adebayo et al., 2022). RFID also makes real-time inventory tracking possible, allowing libraries to conduct stock checks without interrupting operations (Wilson & Carter, 2023). However, the high cost of RFID infrastructure, including tags and readers, can be prohibitive for budget-constrained libraries (Choi & Lee, 2023). For the John Harris Library, RFID could be considered a future enhancement, with the initial focus on software-based automation.
Mobile and web accessibility are critical for modern LMS, given the widespread use of smartphones and tablets. Adebayo et al. (2022) found that mobile access can increase library usage by up to 25%, particularly among students who prefer to search and reserve resources on their phones. Web-based LMS, built on frameworks like React or Angular, offer responsive designs that adapt to various screen sizes, ensuring a consistent user experience (Kumar & Singh, 2021). It is also essential to ensure accessibility for users with disabilities by complying with standards like WCAG 2.1, which includes features like text-to-speech, high-contrast interfaces, and keyboard navigation (Gupta & Sharma, 2021). Multilingual support can further enhance accessibility for international students, a growing demographic at the John Harris Library (Wilson & Carter, 2023). The proposed LMS must prioritize mobile and web accessibility to meet diverse user needs and promote inclusivity.
Data analytics tools in LMS provide insights into resource usage, user behavior, and operational efficiency. Wilson and Carter (2023) emphasize that analytics can inform collection development, identify underutilized resources, and optimize staffing schedules. For example, circulation reports can highlight peak borrowing times, which helps with better resource allocation (Smith & Johnson, 2020). Predictive analytics, powered by ML, can forecast demand for specific materials, which improves inventory management (Choi & Lee, 2023). However, the complexity of these tools may require staff training, which needs to be part of the implementation plan (Patel & Kumar, 2022). For the John Harris Library, basic analytics like circulation and usage reports can provide immediate benefits, with advanced features considered for future phases.

3.3 Analysis of Existing System
3.3.1 Problems of Existing System
The current library management system at the John Harris Library relies heavily on manual record-keeping, which introduces several critical inefficiencies. For instance, handwritten logs and physical ledgers are prone to human errors, such as misentries or illegible handwriting, leading to inaccuracies in book tracking and delays in processing requests. Additionally, tracking borrowed and overdue books is cumbersome, often requiring manual cross-referencing of multiple registers, which can result in overlooked fines or lost items. The system also lacks integration with digital resources, meaning users cannot access e-books or online catalogs seamlessly, limiting the library's ability to support modern research needs. Furthermore, user authentication and security mechanisms are inadequate, with no robust password systems or role-based access controls, making the system vulnerable to unauthorized access and data breaches. These issues collectively contribute to reduced productivity, frustrated users, and potential financial losses from unrecovered materials.

The proposed enhanced LMS introduces a fully automated and integrated platform that revolutionizes library operations. It features automated cataloguing through barcode scanning and RFID integration, allowing librarians to add and update book records efficiently. Borrowing and returning processes are streamlined via self-service kiosks and mobile apps, where users can check out items with minimal staff intervention. A secure login system, utilizing encrypted credentials and multi-factor authentication, ensures that librarians have administrative privileges while students access only user-specific features. Advanced search functionalities enable quick queries by title, author, ISBN, or keywords, with filters for availability and location. Overdue reminders are automated via email and SMS notifications, reducing the administrative burden on staff. Moreover, the system integrates digital resources, providing access to e-books, journals, and online databases within a unified interface. This holistic approach not only addresses the shortcomings of the existing system but also positions the library as a forward-thinking resource hub.
3.3.3 Advantages of Proposed System
The proposed LMS offers numerous advantages that significantly elevate library operations. By automating transactions, it improves efficiency, enabling faster borrowing and returning processes that can handle higher volumes during peak times without delays. Human errors in data management are drastically reduced through digital validation checks and automated backups, ensuring data integrity and reliability. Security and access control are enhanced with role-based permissions and audit logs, protecting sensitive user information from breaches. Users benefit from an enhanced experience, including intuitive search tools that provide real-time results and personalized recommendations, as well as notification features for due dates and reservations. The V-Model was adopted for the development of the enhanced LMS, as it provides a structured framework that emphasizes verification and validation at every stage, aligning development activities with corresponding testing phases (Pandey et al., 2021).
